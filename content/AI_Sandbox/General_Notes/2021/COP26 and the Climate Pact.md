# COP26 and the Climate Pact

<div class="keyword-row">
  <span class="inline-keyword">climate</span>
  <span class="inline-keyword">carbon</span>
  <span class="inline-keyword">transition</span>
  <span class="inline-keyword">netzero</span>

</div>

COP26 ended with a pact that tried to keep the temperature goal alive while acknowledging how hard the path is. The language was softer than many wanted, but the agreement still moved climate policy into a more concrete phase. The question was not just ambition; it was credibility.

I read it as a transition risk signal. Once a global deal exists, capital can price policy risk more aggressively. That changes investment behavior in energy, infrastructure, and commodities. <span class="inline-claim">Climate policy is now a balance-sheet variable</span>.

The other signal was accounting. Targets are only as meaningful as the measurement behind them. The pressure to standardize carbon accounting will grow, and that will reshape how companies report and how investors evaluate them.

## signals
- Policy credibility matters more than headline ambition.
- Carbon accounting will become a competitive constraint.
- Transition risk is now priced into capital decisions.
- Energy systems face a dual mandate: stability and decarbonization.
- Global agreements shape local investment timelines.

## my take
COP26 felt like a waypoint, not a finish line. It gave direction but not certainty. The real work is in enforcement, reporting, and infrastructure buildout. That is where the bottlenecks live.
